Why we never call it a "match"

4 min read · Inside Kindly

Every so often, someone new to Kindly will ask when they'll get "matched" with someone. It's an understandable question — the word is everywhere now, borrowed from apps that use it to describe two profiles that have both swiped right. We always give the same answer: we don't match people. We arrange conversations. The distinction matters more than it might sound.

A word carries its history with it

"Match" comes from a world of algorithms scoring compatibility, of profiles competing for attention, of an outcome measured by whether two people decide to keep talking. That's a perfectly reasonable way to build a dating app. It is not what Kindly is.

When you request a conversation on Kindly, nobody is scored against anybody else. You tell us a topic, and we look for another member who has asked for the same one. There's no swiping, no profile to optimise, and — deliberately — no outcome you're meant to be working towards beyond the conversation itself.

"We don't match people. We arrange conversations. The distinction matters more than it might sound."

Why the language matters

Words shape expectations before anything else does. Call something a match, and people arrive braced for chemistry, for a verdict on whether it "worked." Call it an arranged conversation, and people arrive curious instead — about a person, a topic, an hour that might go somewhere interesting. The second version tends to relax people. The first tends to make them perform.

We've watched this play out enough times to trust it isn't a coincidence. Members who come to Kindly expecting a dating-app dynamic often spend the first ten minutes waiting to see if there's a spark. Members who come expecting a conversation tend to just start one — and, more often than not, they're still talking well past the hour they'd blocked out.

What we're actually building

Kindly exists because good conversation has quietly become one of the harder things to arrange in a full life. Not romance. Not friendship, exactly, in the way that word implies ongoing obligation. Just an hour, on a topic that matters to you, with someone equally glad to have it.

So we'll keep saying "arranged, never automated," and we'll keep avoiding the word that makes this sound like something it isn't. It's a small thing. We think it's also the thing that makes Kindly work.
