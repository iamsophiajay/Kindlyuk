Conversation tips

Seven questions better than "how are you?"

6 min read · Conversation tips

"How are you?" gets answered on autopilot. "Fine, thanks, you?" — and just like that, a conversation is over before it started. We asked the listeners who've had the most conversations through Kindly which opening lines actually get people talking. Here are the seven that come up again and again.

1. "What's something you've changed your mind about recently?"

This one works because it assumes the person is still growing, still thinking. Most people have an answer ready within seconds — and it's rarely small talk once they start explaining why.

2. "What's taking up most of your headspace this week?"

Softer than "what's wrong," broader than "how's work." It leaves room for anything from a deadline to a decision about where to live next.

3. "What's the last thing that made you properly laugh?"

People light up remembering this. It's also a fast way to learn what someone finds funny, which tells you more about them than a job title ever could.

"The best questions don't ask for information. They ask for a story."

4. "If you had a free afternoon with no obligations, what would you actually do?"

Not what they think they should say — what they'd really do. The gap between the two answers is often where the interesting conversation lives.

5. "What's something you're better at now than five years ago?"

A gentle way into someone's sense of progress, without asking them to boast. Most people need a moment to think, and that pause is usually worth waiting for.

6. "What's a place you think about more than you'd expect?"

Sometimes it's a childhood street, sometimes a city they visited once for four days. Either way, you'll usually hear a story you weren't expecting.

7. "What's something you're looking forward to, even a small thing?"

A good closer, and a good opener too. It tends to end conversations on the same warmth they started with — which, at Kindly, is really the whole point.

None of these need a script. Try one, listen properly to the answer, and let the next question follow naturally from what you just heard. That's most of what a good conversation is.
