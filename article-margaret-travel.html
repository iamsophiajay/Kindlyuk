The retired teacher who talks travel every Tuesday

5 min read · Member stories

Margaret, 67, taught art in Edinburgh for thirty-one years. She retired four years ago, and for the first eighteen months of it, she says, her days had a shape she didn't much like — full of things to do, oddly quiet all the same.

"I hadn't realised how much of my conversation used to happen by accident," she says. "The staff room. The corridor. Someone popping their head round the door. You don't notice it's the thing holding your week together until it's gone."

An hour that became a habit

Margaret joined Kindly on her daughter's suggestion, mostly to be agreeable rather than because she expected much from it. Her first conversation was about travel — a topic she picked, she admits, almost at random. She was arranged an hour with a member in his thirties who'd just come back from six months in South America.

"I thought I'd last twenty minutes," she says. "We went on for nearly ninety. He wanted to know about a trip I'd taken to Lisbon in 1988, on my own, which nobody in my life has asked me about in decades. I wanted to know everything about Bolivia. It didn't feel like an interview. It felt like the good kind of conversation you have with someone at a wedding you've only just met, except better, because neither of us had anywhere else to be."

"It didn't feel like an interview. It felt like the good kind of conversation you have with someone at a wedding you've only just met."

Thirty conversations and counting

That was eight months ago. Since then, Margaret has arranged a conversation nearly every Tuesday — always travel, always with someone new. She's talked to a marine biologist about the Faroe Islands, a chef about street food in Bangkok, and a fellow retiree about a road trip through Namibia that she now, only half-joking, calls research for a trip of her own.

"I'm not looking for anything in particular," she says, when asked what keeps her coming back. "I just like knowing that on a Tuesday, there's an hour where someone wants to hear what I think, and I want to hear what they think. That's rarer than people admit."

What she'd tell someone considering it

"Pick something you actually care about, not something you think sounds impressive," she says. "And don't plan too much of what you're going to say. The best hour I've had so far started with me admitting I'd never actually seen the Northern Lights despite living in Scotland my whole life. We ended up talking about disappointment for forty minutes. I wasn't expecting that when I sat down."

Margaret's next conversation is booked for Tuesday. The topic, as ever, is travel. Who she'll be talking to, she doesn't yet know — and that, she says, is rather the point.
